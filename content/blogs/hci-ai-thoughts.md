There's a version of AI research that treats humans as an afterthought. The model is the thing; users are just the deployment surface. I understand why — the technical problems are genuinely hard and genuinely interesting on their own terms.

But I keep getting pulled toward a different question: not just *can the model do this*, but *does the model help the person do this better?* Those are not the same question, and the gap between them is where I want to spend my career.

## Where This Came From

I didn't arrive at HCI through a clean intellectual journey. It started with frustration.

I was tutoring ESL students — bright people, working hard — and watching them interrupt their reading every few sentences to look something up. By the time they returned to the text, the thread of the argument was gone. The vocabulary lookup had succeeded; the reading had failed.

That observation led me to Marginalia, my annotation interface for ESL readers. But more importantly, it taught me to ask: *what does the person actually need in this moment?* That question is cognitive science before it's computer science.

## The Bidirectional Relationship

The research I've read — Microsoft's work on human-agent collaboration, Jeff Bigham's CodeA11y, Fudan's work on contextualizing emotional reactions through multimodal LLMs — points to something I find genuinely exciting: AI and HCI are in a bidirectional relationship.

Advances in AI enable new kinds of interaction that weren't possible before. But HCI research shapes what AI systems are built *for* — what success even means when a system is deployed in a human context.

If you only work on the AI side, you're optimizing for metrics that may or may not translate into human benefit. If you only work on the HCI side, you risk designing for cognitive constraints that AI could actually help lift.

The interesting work happens when you hold both questions at once.

## Where Psychology Comes In

My interdisciplinary coursework in psychology wasn't a detour — it was an asset. Concepts like cognitive load, mental models, and emotion regulation aren't soft add-ons to technical work. They're precise claims about how humans process information, make decisions, and respond to interfaces.

My capstone on emotion detection via wearable devices is an example of this synthesis: it's machine learning, but the problem framing comes entirely from affective science. Which signals correlate with which emotional states, and why, is a psychology question. The model answers it with data.

## What I Actually Want to Build

I want to build systems where the technology recedes — where the user is absorbed in the task, not the tool. That's a high bar. It requires both the AI capability to handle complexity quietly and the HCI discipline to design interactions that feel invisible.

I don't think this is a niche interest. As AI becomes more capable, the question of how it fits into human workflows becomes more urgent, not less. The tools we build in the next few years will shape how people learn, communicate, and understand themselves. Getting that right feels like the work worth doing.
