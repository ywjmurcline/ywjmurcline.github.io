I've been doing stand-up comedy on and off since my second year of university. I attempted to start a stand-up group at SJTU, which failed gently. I then joined the one at Fudan as the sole SJTU member, which succeeded less gently (I was heckled twice).

I co-directed a musical. I served as VP of my school's music club. None of this is on my CV in the way technical projects are. But it informs how I think about design more than I expected.

## The Audience Is Always Right (About How They Feel)

In comedy, the laugh is the only metric. You can explain why the joke was funny — the subverted expectation, the callback, the rhythmic build — and none of it matters if people didn't laugh. The audience's response is the ground truth.

This sounds obvious. But it's surprisingly hard to internalize when you've worked hard on something. The natural impulse is to think: *they didn't get it.* Maybe they didn't. But that's your problem to solve, not theirs.

The same thing is true in UX. Users will navigate your interface in ways that seem wrong to you. They'll miss the button you designed to be prominent. They'll click the thing you thought no one would click. The design is wrong — not the user.

What comedy gives you is practice at receiving that feedback without defensiveness. You bomb, you note what bombed, you try something different next time.

## Timing Is Everything — And Impossible to Fully Specify

In a five-minute set, timing matters at every level. The pause before the punchline. The rhythm of a triple. The decision to speed up or slow down based on the room's energy.

You can't write timing into the script. You can approximate it, develop instincts, rehearse. But the actual timing happens live, in response to a specific audience in a specific mood.

UI animation has this quality. You can specify that a transition takes 200ms with an ease-out curve. But whether that feels right depends on the context — the size of the element, what comes before, what the user was just doing. The spec is the approximation; the experience is the thing.

I spent embarrassing hours tweaking animation timing in Marginalia. I couldn't always articulate why 180ms felt right and 220ms felt sluggish. But I could feel it — and I think that sense came from the same place as knowing when to let a beat breathe in a set.

## Cut Everything That Doesn't Serve the Moment

The discipline that comedy forces on writing is ruthlessness. Every word either earns its place or goes. A joke that's "pretty good" in the middle of a tight set is worse than nothing — it breaks rhythm and spends audience goodwill.

This translates almost directly to interface design. Every element on a screen is a bid for the user's attention. Most bids are not worth making. The information architecture question is always: what does this user need *right now*, in this moment of their task? Everything else is noise.

The hardest part — in comedy and in design — is killing things you like. The observation that took you ten minutes to craft. The secondary action button that might occasionally be useful. The design principle is "kill your darlings." The comedy version is "if you're attached to it, it's probably the problem."

## What I Actually Took Away

I'm not claiming comedy and UX are the same. The stakes are different, the feedback loops are different, the skills are distinct.

But creative practices that demand real-time audience awareness, iterative refinement, and a willingness to let the work be wrong — those build muscles that transfer. I'm a better designer for having bombed in front of forty people at Fudan on a Wednesday night.

I'm not sure how to put that on a CV. But it's true.
